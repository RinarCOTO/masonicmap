Swedish - American Hall

Link copied!

The number of Scandinavian people nearly doubled in San Francisco between 1900 and 1910, with much of that community centered around the area near Market Street and Dolores. Smack dab in the center of it was the Swedish American Hall, built in 1908 and home to several social and fraternal groups. That included, from 1908 until 1987, the Masonic lodge of Balder No. 393, named for the Norse god of light, comprised almost entirely of Scandinavian members. Initially the group aimed to form as a Swedish-language lodge (to complement the existing French, Italian, and German Masonic lodges in San Francisco at the time). Ultimately the group settled on English, however, but still kept a particular Masonic heritage alive for nearly 80 years.

King Solomon’s Temple, Golden Gate Commandery Asylum, Knights Templar

Link copied!

The commandery of Golden Gate No. 16 of the Knights Templar met in the imposing Matthew O’Brien and Carl Werner-designed temple in the Western Addition from 1905–1949. But that was only the beginning of the building’s history: At that point, it was taken over by Macedonia Bapti, st Church, an influential institution during the Civil Rights era at which Martin Luther King twice gave speeches. The imposing façade, built in the so-called “Jacobean phase of the Medieval Revival” style, originally hosted several blue lodges, particularly after the 1906 earthquake, including Occidental No. 22, Argonaut No. 461, Bethlehem No. 453, Educator No. 554, Military Service No. 570, and others. In 1950, the commandery moved to Burlingame.

Richmond Masonic Temple

Link copied!

Many of the fraternal flourishes remain inside the 1908 Richmond Masonic Temple on Arguello Boulevard (originally called First Avenue), designed by Hermann Barth, architect of the city’s German Hospital. Engraved Masonic symbols, Corinthian column paneling, and the vaulted ceiling still give a sense of grandeur to what is now a fitness gym. However, the most striking details of the massive building, which was the first major construction project in the Richmond District after the 1906 earthquake, is not part of the original design at all: In 1936, Bernard J. Joseph (who also designed the old Orpheum Theater, on O’Farrell) renovated the building’s exterior in a Mayan Deco theme, with engraved paneling along the parapet at the building’s roofline. The temple was once home to Richmond No. 375 and later Lebanon No. 495 and Seal Rock No. 536.
